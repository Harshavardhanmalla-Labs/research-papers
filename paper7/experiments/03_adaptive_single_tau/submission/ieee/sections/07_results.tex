\subsection{Change-Point Decisions}

The detector fires at $11$ of $15$ post-W1 windows in the sweep ($73\%$).
Per-cell firing fractions: $K = 50$: $3/5$ ($60\%$); $K = 100$:
$2/5$ ($40\%$); $K = 200$: $4/5$ ($80\%$). The firing pattern is
\textit{not} monotone in capacity; $K = 100$ is the local minimum.

The detector fires \textit{at every cell} at W2 (cp delta
$= +0.144$ everywhere because that magnitude is the W1-to-W2 jump
between the fixed-baseline reference and the lag-1 fit, which is
common across cells). Beyond W2, the per-cell fire pattern reflects
window-specific calibration-target dynamics.

\subsection{H1 --- Adaptive Avoids the K=200 Hazard (Supported)}

At $K = 200$ the per-window deltas $(\textsf{adaptive} -
\textsf{fixed})$ are $0, +3.4, +0.5, +0.5, +0.4, +0.0$~pp at
$w = 1, \ldots, 6$. None of the five post-W1 windows falls below
the pre-registered $-0.01$ tolerance. H1 is supported. This is the
\textit{first} procedure in the Paper~7--8--10 sequence that does
not break at $K = 200$.

The mechanism is straightforward. At $K = 200$ the detector fires
at four of five windows; in those windows \textsf{adaptive} reverts
to \textsf{fixed} and incurs no calibration penalty. At the one
window where the detector does not fire ($w = 4$), the lag-1 fit
happens to be benign (delta vs fixed $= +0.5$~pp, not a harm). The
procedure effectively gates lag-1 to windows where it is safe.

\subsection{H2 --- Moderate-Capacity Penalty (Rejected)}

At $K = 50$, lag-1 averaged $+3.1$~pp over fixed in Paper~7. With
\textsf{adaptive}, three of the five post-W1 windows are gated to
\textsf{fixed} and the cell-mean delta drops to $+3.1$~pp vs fixed
but $-5.3$~pp vs lag-1. Five of five post-W1 windows fail the H2
$\pm 2$~pp tolerance.

At $K = 100$ the situation is reversed: only two windows fire and
the adaptive cell mean is $-0.4$~pp below lag-1 ($\rho_{\text{adapt}}
= 0.89$ vs lag-1's Paper~7 value of $0.99$). H2 is technically
rejected because some individual windows still exceed the $\pm
2$~pp tolerance, but the penalty is small at this capacity.

The H2 rejection is the principled cost of the H1 success. A
procedure that gates lag-1 to safe windows necessarily gives up some
of lag-1's recovery in windows where the lag-1 fit was already
working. The K=50 penalty is the price the threshold $\tau = 0.05$
extracts.

\subsection{H3 --- Firing Not Monotone in K (Rejected)}

Spearman$(K, \text{fire fraction})$ is $+0.5$ ($p = 0.67$), well
below the pre-registered $+0.8$ threshold. The fire fractions are
$\{0.6, 0.4, 0.8\}$ at $K \in \{50, 100, 200\}$.

The non-monotone pattern reflects window-specific dynamics in the
calibration-target P@50 stream rather than a clean capacity
ordering. At $K = 100$ the W3, W4, and W6 windows have small
between-window cp\_deltas ($-0.004, -0.024, +0.024$) and do not fire;
at $K = 50$ the W6 window happens to have a larger $\Delta$
($+0.108$) and does fire. The detector responds to the actual
distributional shift, not to capacity directly, and the relationship
between capacity and shift is non-linear in this regime.

\subsection{H4 --- Adaptive Marginally Beats Static Gate (Supported)}

At $K = 200$, cell-mean \textsf{adaptive} = $0.273$ vs cell-mean
\textsf{gated} = $0.266$, a margin of $+0.7$~pp. H4 is supported.

The margin is small. At $K = 200$ the \textsf{gated} rule reverts to
\textsf{fixed} unconditionally; \textsf{adaptive} reverts at four of
five windows and uses lag-1 at the one window ($w = 4$) where the
detector does not fire. The lag-1 fit at $w = 4$ adds $+0.5$~pp over
fixed, and this small contribution drives the H4 margin. A
practitioner who values simplicity may prefer the static gate; one
who values the marginal per-window adaptivity may prefer
\textsf{adaptive}.
