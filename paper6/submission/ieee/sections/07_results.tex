\subsection{Universal EPSS Decay}

\input{tables/tab_decay_slopes}

Table~\ref{tab:slopes} and Fig.~\ref{fig:slopes} show the per-cell mean
P@50 decay slope. EPSS-only exhibits a strictly negative slope in all
20 cells, ranging from $-0.025$ (at $K = 200, \lambda = 12$) to $-0.052$
(at $K = 50, \lambda = 3$). No cell in the grid produces a steady-state
EPSS regime (slope $\geq -0.01$); pre-registered hypothesis H3

\subsection{Predicted Monotonicity Rejected (H1, H2)}

The pre-registered prediction was that EPSS decay slope would scale
monotonically more-negative with $K$ at every fixed $\lambda$ (H1) and
monotonically less-negative with $\lambda$ at every fixed $K$ (H2), with
Spearman $|\rho| \geq 0.8$ required for ``supported''.

For H1, Spearman$(K, \mathrm{slope})$ at fixed $\lambda$ yields
$\rho = 0.30$ at every $\lambda \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$ --- this fails the
pre-registered $|\rho| \geq 0.8$ threshold and carries the
\textit{wrong sign}. The
non-monotonicity is visible in Table~\ref{tab:slopes}: at $\lambda = 1$,
slopes are $-0.038$ ($K = 10$), $-0.047$ ($K = 25$), $-0.051$ ($K = 50$),
$-0.043$ ($K = 100$), $-0.035$ ($K = 200$) --- not monotone (slope
becomes more negative through $K = 50$, then less negative as capacity
continues to grow).

For H2, Spearman$(\lambda, \mathrm{slope})$ at fixed $K$ yields
$\rho \in \{0.6, 0.6, -0.4, 0.0, 1.0\}$ for $K \in \{10, 25, 50, 100, 200\}$;
only $K = 200$ meets the threshold. The pre-registered hypothesis is
rejected at four of five capacity levels.

The honest interpretation is that the (K, $\lambda$) interaction in the
synthetic regime studied is not a clean monotone scaling. Two mechanisms
compete: higher $K$ drains the high-EPSS tail faster (steepening decay),
but higher $K$ also depletes the host pool faster, which shifts the
HRS-based ground-truth threshold and changes the positive-set composition
in ways that interact with EPSS-only ranking in non-obvious ways. The
sweep captures the net effect rather than rationalising it.

\subsection{HygienePrio Also Collapses (H4 Rejected)}

\input{tables/tab_w6_retention}

Table~\ref{tab:w6} and Fig.~\ref{fig:retention} report W6 mean P@50 per
cell for HygienePrio-full and EPSS-only. At $K \leq 50$, HygienePrio-full
holds $\geq 0.473$ at every cell --- the regime Paper~5~\cite{paper5temporal}
reported. At $K = 100$, HygienePrio-full's W6 P@50 sits in
$[0.315, 0.334]$ across $\lambda$; at $K = 200$, it falls to $[0.062, 0.116]$.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig2_w6_retention.pdf}
\caption{HygienePrio-full Window-6 mean P@50 across the $(K, \lambda)$
grid. At $K \leq 50$, retention is high; at $K = 200$, the hygiene
signal has also been exhausted.}
\label{fig:retention}
\end{figure}

The pre-registered hypothesis H4 (W6 mean P@50 $\geq 0.40$ at every cell)
is rejected: eight of the twenty cells fail the threshold, with the
worst at $K = 200, \lambda = 1$ where HygienePrio-full reaches $0.062$.
The mechanism is symmetric to EPSS-only's compositional decay: when
remediation capacity greatly exceeds new-CVE arrival, the
high-HRS-host portion of the residual backlog is depleted, the
recomputed-per-window HRS distribution flattens, and the binary
ground-truth condition is satisfied by an increasingly small
positive set with no discriminative structure that HRS or HygienePrio
can recover.

\subsection{Per-Pair Dominance Persists}

Across the full 15{,}000-row sweep, HygienePrio-full outperforms
EPSS-only at P@50 in \textbf{2{,}881 of 3{,}000 (cell, seed, window)
comparisons (96.0\%)}. The minimum per-cell dominance fraction is
$76.7\%$. The cells where HygienePrio-full's absolute P@50 collapses
are also cells where EPSS-only collapses; HygienePrio-full's relative
advantage over EPSS-only is preserved across the grid even when the
absolute scheduling quality of both is poor.

\subsection{Corner Trajectories}

\begin{figure}[t]

Fig.~\ref{fig:corners} shows P@50 trajectories at the four corner
cells. The top row ($K = 10$) reproduces Paper~5's qualitative pattern:
HygienePrio-full is stable, EPSS-only decays, HRS-only sits between,
while CVSS-only tracks the Random floor. The bottom row ($K = 200$) shows the previously
unreported collapse pattern: at the highest capacity, all hygiene-
and exploit-likelihood-based methods decay together toward the
random floor.
